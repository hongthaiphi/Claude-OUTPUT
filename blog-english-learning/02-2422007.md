**EN — Chapter 2: a rough leg of the journey**
A bit more about Vinh and Hiền — both close friends and old neighbors. On the night of the 7th I slept over at Vinh's place to leave early the next morning. Hiền's plan was that a car would come right to the house and pick us up to Hanoi. Now, you have to understand — after the nightmare of waiting for buses, getting overcharged, and being crammed onto a packed coach on the 6th, the idea of a car coming to pick us up, with proper seats, at a surprisingly cheap price, had me too excited to sleep. But alas, "life isn't as sweet as you dream it." At 4:55am the phone rang loudly — I thought it was Hiền saying the car had arrived. I threw off the blanket, jumped out of bed, grabbed my bag, ready to head out — and Vinh's dad, who answered the phone, delivered some brutal news: the car had taken a different job, no longer carrying passengers, wait until 11:30 for the noon trip instead. Well, okay, wait it is. Vinh and I rolled right back into bed.

The morning passed in a haze. Ate an early lunch to be ready by 11, when Hiền's dad would drive us both to the town to catch a bus. At 11:10 we started waiting — and waiting, and waiting, no sign of the car we'd arranged. Called it, phone off. Dog dead, that's that. The car had completely vanished. We waited and waited for a coach, but none of them looked like they had room — way too crowded. Decided to take the Sao Đỏ–Bắc Ninh bus instead, and honestly, brilliant decision. Roomy, comfortable, breezy. Got to Bắc Ninh and the 204 bus was jam-packed, with this mysterious hazy smoke inside — Hiền and I decided: "get off, catch the next one." But fortune favors the good — a coach competing with the bus route waved us over, plenty of empty seats, hopped on, and it flew straight to Gia Lâm. Shame it didn't actually pull into the Gia Lâm station, just dropped us on the road outside, costing us another 5k for a motorbike taxi into the station — and the next coach, number 22, was already packed solid. Not playing with that chaos. Waited for the next one, and luckily didn't have to wait long — 5 minutes later another came. So all three legs with Hiền, we got seats. Dropped Hiền off at home in Triều Khúc, and then it was just me — walked to the bus stop and headed home. My shoulders were wrecked from carrying bags with Hiền, so I decided that once I got to Thủy Lợi, I'd take a motorbike taxi, no walking. Damn, 8k for a motorbike ride from the Thủy Lợi gate to Vĩnh Hồ — normally I walk that on my way to school, but today I was too loaded down. Eh, whatever, money's fleeting anyway, haha. Comrade Thái has made it back to "headquarters" — what horrors await our loyal comrade? Read on for chapter 3...

**EN — Final chapter: the computer problem**
No mouse, no keyboard — both taken back to my hometown. Borrowed a fancy USB IBM keyboard from Hà, but my ancient computer turned its nose up at it, wouldn't recognize it, ugh, couldn't even turn the computer on. Had to trek all the way down to Lương Thế Vinh to buy a whole new mouse and keyboard set. Plugged it in, still nothing. Thought the PS/2 ports on the motherboard were dead, nearly threw myself on the bed and bawled. But — never surrender to circumstance, haha. Swapped them to the right ports, worked instantly — otherwise how would I be writing this right now, haha.

Speaking of computers — since I couldn't get online at home, I went to the net café (the same one whose internet I'd been piggybacking at home). Wow — I'd actually thought about proposing that myself before, not because I needed the side income but because I needed the internet access, and once I got internet at home I dropped the idea. Now she's offering it to me, and not even asking about last month's internet bill — sure, why not. But this lady's sharp, she wanted to "test my skills" first. And she put me through the wringer, ugh, poor me — I only really enjoy installing software, operating systems, utilities, apps, but fixing a mouse, wiring up headphones? No way.
